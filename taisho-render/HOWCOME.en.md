# HOWCOME

## Why this exists

This project began from a simple experience:

Seeing fragments of the Jingo-ji Issaikyo.

Not as a complete canon,  
but as scattered presences —  
a single scroll behind glass,  
a few images in a museum,  
restricted access in temple archives.

The original work was never meant to be seen this way.

It was created as a whole.

Carefully copied, line by line,  
as an act of devotion.

Gold on dark paper.  
Structured, deliberate, and precise.  
Not just text — but Buddhist visual culture.

---

## The gap

Today, that completeness is no longer accessible.

- Collections are fragmented  
- Institutions show only selected pieces  
- Physical access is limited  
- A full set is practically impossible to experience  

What remains is not the canon as a whole,  
but traces of it.

---

## What this project does

This is not a reproduction.

It does not attempt to replace the original.

Instead, it asks:

> What happens if the canon is reconstructed as a system?

Using the Taisho Tripitaka as a textual base,  
this project builds a rendering pipeline:

- XML → text  
- text → normalized characters  
- characters → grid  
- grid → image  

Each page is generated, not scanned.

A synthetic reconstruction of a lost spatial experience.

---

## Digital offering

This work is also a form of digital offering.

Not in material form,  
but through process.

Repetition, structure, and rendering  
mirror the act of copying scriptures —  
translated into code.

---

## On authenticity

The original manuscripts remain irreplaceable.

This project does not compete with them.

It exists alongside them,  
as a different way of seeing:

- not preservation  
- not simulation  
- but interpretation  

---

## What comes next

A viewer is planned.

Not just to display images,  
but to restore a sense of scale and immersion:

- zoomable pages  
- continuous reading  
- spatial navigation  

An attempt to bring back  
the experience of encountering the canon —  
not as fragments,  
but as a field.

---

## Closing

This began with a single impression:

That something complete  
had become impossible to see.

This project is an attempt  
to make that completeness imaginable again.
